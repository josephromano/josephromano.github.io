\section{Alternating current (AC) circuits}

%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\subsection{Individual $R$, $L$, and $C$ circuits}
\bi

\i Consider a circuit driven by the $AC$ potential
${\cal E}(t) = {\cal E}_m\sin(\omega_d t)$, with driving angular frequency $\omega_d$.

\i Then the instantaneous current $i(t)$ through the circuit will also vary sinusoidally
with angular frequency $\omega_d$.
But it will be shifted in phase relative to ${\cal E}(t)$, 
so that $i(t) = I \sin(\omega_d t - \phi)$.

\i Using the loop rule for single element $R$, $L$, and $C$ circuits:
%
\ben
\i Purely resistive circuit: $\phi=0$ and $I = {\cal E}_m/R$.
\i Purely inductive circuit: $\phi=\pi/2$ and $I = {\cal E}_m/X_L$, where $X_L \equiv L\,\omega_d$.
\i Purely capacitive circuit: $\phi=-\pi/2$ and $I = {\cal E}_m/X_C$, where $X_C \equiv 1/(C\omega_d)$.
\een

\i The above results imply that 
$i$ and ${\cal E}$ are in phase for a purely resistive circuit;
${\cal E}$ leads $i$ by $\pi/2$ for a purely inductive circuit; 
and $i$ leads ${\cal E}$ by $\pi/2$ for a purely capacitive circuit.

\i The quantities $X_L$ and $X_C$ are inductive and capacitive {\it reactances}.
These quantities generalizes the resistance $R$ to inductors and capacitors in 
AC circuits.
Note that $X_L=0$ and $X_C\rightarrow\infty$ for a DC circuit (i.e., $\omega_d=0$).

\ei

\subsection{Single $RLC$ circuit in series}

\bi
\i We now consider the driving potential ${\cal E}(t)$ connected to 
a series $RLC$ circuit.
The loop rule yields:
\be
{\cal E} = iR + L\frac{\dd i}{\dd t} + \frac{1}{C}\int^t \> i(t')\dd t'
=I\left[R\sin(\omega_d t-\phi) + (X_L-X_C)\cos(\omega_d t- \phi) \right]
\ee

\i Setting the lhs to ${\cal E}_m \sin(\omega_d t)$ and using the 
trig identities
%
\be
\sin(A\pm B) = \sin A\cos B\pm \cos A\sin B\,,\qquad
\cos(A\pm B) = \cos A\cos B\mp \sin A\sin B
\ee
%
to expand $\sin(\omega_d t-\phi)$ and $\cos(\omega_d t-\phi)$ on the rhs, 
we find
%
\be
\tan\phi = \frac{X_L-X_C}{R}\,,
\quad{\rm and}\quad
I = {\cal E}_m/{Z}\,
\quad{\rm where}\quad
Z = \sqrt{R^2 + (X_L-X_C)^2}
\ee
%

\i $Z$ is called the {\it impedance} of the $RLC$ circuit. 
Its value depends on the driving frequency $\omega_d$.

\i Resonance occurs when the impedance $Z$ is as small as possible.
This occurs when the driving frequency $\omega_d$ is chosen so that
the capacitive and inductive reactances $X_C$ and $X_L$ cancel one another:
\be
Z = R
\quad\iff\quad
X_L = X_C
\quad\iff\quad
\omega_d = \frac{1}{\sqrt{LC}}
\ee
%
This is the natural frequency for a simple $LC$ oscillator circuit.

\i For this choice of $\omega_d$, the amplitude of the current $I$ 
is as large as possible ($I = {\cal E}_m/R$) for fixed ${\cal E}_m$.

\ei

\subsection{Transformers}

\bi

\i Consider a transformer constructed from two coils of wire
$N_1$ and $N_2$ connected by an iron core.
The iron core ``links" the magnetic fluxes passing through 
the two coils so that $\Phi_{12}=\Phi_{21}\equiv \Phi$.

\i Connect the ``primary" coil (side 1) of the transformer to 
AC potential ${\cal E}_1$ and the ``secondary" coil (side 2) of 
the transformer to a switch $S$ and resistance $R_2$.
Then
%
\be
{\cal E}_1 = -N_1 \frac{\dd \Phi}{\dd t}\,,
\quad
{\cal E}_2 = -N_2 \frac{\dd \Phi}{\dd t}
\quad\implies\quad
{\cal E}_2 = \left(\frac{N_2}{N_1}\right){\cal E}_1
\ee

\i Conservation of energy 
${\cal E}_1 i_1= {\cal E}_2 i_2$ then implies
$i_2 = (N_1/N_2) i_1$, and an ``effective" resistance
$R_1 = (N_1/N_2)^2 R_2$ seen by ${\cal E}_1$,
using $i_1^2 R_1 = i_2^2 R_2$ to define $R_1$.

\ei
